There's an unspoken expectation that by your mid-20s, you should know who you are. You've finished school (maybe), you have some work experience, you've been through enough to have opinions. You're an adult now.

So why does it feel like everyone else got the memo and you didn't?

## The Gap Between Who You Are and Who You're Supposed to Be

Most people in their 20s are managing a quiet tension: the version of themselves they're presenting to the world — competent, directional, fine — and the version that shows up at 2am wondering if any of it is right.

The career that looked good on paper. The relationship that checks all the boxes but feels hollow. The creeping sense that you've been building someone else's life.

This isn't a crisis in the clinical sense. It's a signal.

## Why This Decade Is Actually Designed for This

Your 20s are the last major developmental window before identity solidifies. The brain is still forming — literally. The choices you make now carry more weight than they will later, which is exactly why they feel so heavy.

This isn't a flaw in your design. It's the design.

The problem is that no one tells you that the discomfort is normal — or that it's pointing somewhere.

## What Gets in the Way

A few things tend to keep people stuck:

**Comparison.** Social media shows you highlight reels. Everyone else's path looks more intentional than yours feels from the inside.

**Avoidance.** It's easier to stay busy than to sit with the question of whether you're building something you actually want.

**Waiting for certainty.** Many people believe they should feel sure before they move. But clarity almost always comes *through* movement, not before it.

## A Different Way to Approach It

Instead of asking "who am I?" — which is enormous and often paralyzing — try asking smaller questions:

- What did I do last week that felt genuinely like me?
- What am I tolerating that I'd stop if I thought I deserved better?
- If no one was watching, what would I spend more time on?

These aren't rhetorical. They're the beginning of a real answer.
